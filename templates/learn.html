CORE CONCEPT

Horizontal (HBB) vs. Oriented (OBB)

Traditional object detection uses Horizontal Bounding Boxes (HBB). While effective for street-level images, they struggle with the arbitrary angles found in drone imagery.

[Image of Horizontal vs Oriented Bounding Boxes on a diagonal ship]

Background Contamination

HBBs capture significant background noise like water or grass, which "contaminates" the model's feature extraction.

NMS Conflict

In dense scenes, overlapping horizontal boxes cause Non-Maximum Suppression (NMS) to accidentally delete valid detections.

TECHNICAL SPECS

The 5-Parameter Prediction Model

Standard detectors use 4 values. Our orientation-aware model predicts a 5th parameter: Theta (θ).

[Image of Oriented Bounding Box geometry showing x, y, width, height, and rotation angle theta]
  • x, y: The precise center coordinates of the object.
  • w, h: Width and height aligned to the object's axis, not the image axis.
  • θ (Theta): The rotation angle, allowing the box to "tilt" for a perfect fit.
METRICS

Rotated Intersection over Union (RIoU)

Accuracy is measured by the overlap between the prediction and the ground truth. In OBB detection, we treat boxes as polygons.

Rotated IoU calculates the intersection area of two rotated rectangles. This ensures that even a location-perfect box is penalized if its angle is slightly off, forcing high precision.

DATASETS & LIMITS

Aerial Challenges

Detecting objects from high altitudes introduces unique data problems:

Scale Variation: Images can contain a 10px car and a 500px bridge; we use Feature Pyramid Networks (FPN) to detect both.
Perspective Ambiguity: Objects look different from above. Models must learn "top-down" visual signatures rather than typical profiles.
